\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:disc}
\label{sec:conc}

LexUR provides a robust, certifiable decision rule for multicriteria analysis. While average-case methods like the weighted sum or SMAA excel when decision makers seek typical performance across assumed probability distributions, LexUR is designed for scenarios where the decision maker cannot state precise trade-offs and must defend a single robust choice against the worst-case disappointment over a declared family of rational interpretations. 

It accomplishes this by turning the candidate set into a lexicographically optimal compromise, accompanied by an auditable certificate that explicitly names the most severely binding probes. The empirical evidence across extensive benchmark geometries, preference families, and noisy configurations demonstrates that LexUR matches the tail-loss robustness of the strongest scalarizations (like Savage minimax regret) while dramatically reducing bias from redundant criteria.

However, the framework has clear, declared limitations. Because LexUR normalises based on the empirical extremes of the candidate set, it violates Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives. Adding or removing unselected points can shift the active nadir, causing rank reversal in the final recommendation. Therefore, if the true normalisation bounds are uncertain, practitioners must employ interval estimates and report the resulting stability classes rather than relying on point-estimate selections. Additionally, LexUR trades average-case performance for its worst-case robustness.

Ultimately, LexUR replaces the traditional efficient set—a mere safety filter—with a certified recommendation. By relocating modelling choices into an explicit family of monotone questions, it gives decision makers an explainable, low-disappointment compromise that transparently reports its own fragility.
