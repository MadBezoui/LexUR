\section{Extensions}
\label{sec:ext}

\subsection{Stochastic regret certificates}
When criteria are noisy, we model $f_i(x)$ as drawn from a joint distribution over scenarios. Each probe value is a random variable, and we apply a generic monotone risk functional $\rho$ (e.g., sample average or Conditional Value-at-Risk) directly to the probe value $q(r(x,\omega))$. The normalisation anchors are taken consistently on this functional. We define the stochastic regret as
\begin{equation}
\reg_q^\rho(x)=\frac{\rho_q(x)-\rho_q^\star}{\rho_q^{\mathrm w}-\rho_q^\star}.
\end{equation}

For the empirical experiments in \S\ref{sec:exp}, we evaluate a specific \emph{confidence-aware} variant using upper-confidence bounds $\tilde f_i(x)=\hat\mu_i(x)+z_{\gamma}\hat\sigma_i(x)/\sqrt{S}$ before applying the deterministic LexUR rule. While this heuristic provides practical risk control, the empirical standard deviation is not pointwise monotone, meaning exact scenario-wise Pareto compatibility is not strictly preserved by this specific statistical variant. 

For theoretically strict risk control, a monotone functional like CVaR must be used instead. The stochastic framework preserves Pareto compatibility and recovers the true winner asymptotically under standard margin conditions, as formalised below (proofs in appendix).

\begin{proposition}[Scenario-wise Pareto compatibility]
\label{thm:stoch}
For finite $A$, finite $\Q$, and sample-average probe means, if $x$ weakly dominates $y$ in every scenario and strictly dominates it in at least one criterion for at least one scenario, then the sample-average LexUR rule does not prefer $y$ to $x$. If $\Q$ separates criteria and the dominance is strict on a separated coordinate, $x$ is strictly preferred.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Sample-average consistency]
\label{thm:stoch2}
If the population tolerance-LexUR winner satisfies the positive margin condition of Theorem~\ref{thm:stab}, and population probe ranges are non-degenerate, then the sample-average LexUR winner converges in probability to the population winner as the sample size $S\to\infty$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Multi-stakeholder aggregation}
While primarily a single decision-maker tool, LexUR can be extended to multi-stakeholder settings. If $U$ indexes stakeholders, each assigning an importance weight to the criteria, one can define a composite family of stakeholder-specific probes. Lexicographic minimisation over this expanded family implements a Rawlsian maximin logic \citep{rawls1971}, protecting the most severely disappointed stakeholder on their most critical concerns. We leave the formal development and evaluation of this extension to future work.
