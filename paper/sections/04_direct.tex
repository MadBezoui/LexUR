\section{Computation}
\label{sec:direct}

LexUR is primarily a candidate-set selection rule. We present the exact finite algorithm, followed by a brief summary of how it can be formulated directly on a continuous feasible set.

\subsection{Exact algorithm for finite candidate sets}

The finite-set procedure is summarised in Figure~\ref{alg:finite}. With naive probe evaluation, producing a complete lexicographic ranking of all candidates costs $O(NKm + NK\log K + NK\log N)$, where $N$ is the number of candidates, $m$ is the number of criteria, and $K$ is the number of probes.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\fbox{
\begin{minipage}{0.9\textwidth}
\textbf{Procedure: Exact finite-set LexUR ($\tau=0$)}\vspace{2mm}\\
\textbf{1. Dominance filter:} Remove any Pareto-dominated $x \in A$.\\
\textbf{2. Normalisation:} Find active range $\ideal_i, \nadir_i$ and compute $r_i(x)$.\\
\textbf{3. Anchor bounds:} Find $q^\star = \min q(r(x))$ and $q^{\mathrm w} = \max q(r(x))$.\\
\textbf{4. Regret evaluation:} Compute $\reg_q(x) = (q(r(x)) - q^\star) / (q^{\mathrm w} - q^\star)$.\\
\textbf{5. Certificate sorting:} Sort the labelled pairs $(\reg_q(x),q)$ descending to obtain $\reg^{\downarrow}(x)$.\\
\textbf{6. Exact selection:} Return every candidate attaining the lexicographic minimum.\\
\textbf{7. Numerical reporting:} Report the $\tau$-stability class.
\end{minipage}
}
\caption{Finite-set LexUR procedure.}
\label{alg:finite}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Continuous formulations}

\begin{proposition}[Front-free continuous computation]
Lexicographic minimisation of the sorted regret vector over a continuous set $\X$ is exactly equivalent to sequentially minimising the cumulative sums of the $p$ largest disappointments, which admits an exact linearisation for structured problems.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Computational bottleneck]
Under structured settings, the required anchors can be computed directly, so LexUR may be applied without explicit front generation. However, computing the per-probe anchor $q^{\mathrm w} = \max_{x \in \X} q(r(x))$ requires maximising a convex function over a convex set. These $2K$ global anchor subproblems may be no cheaper than front generation in the general case. We provide the full continuous derivations in the appendix.
\end{remark}
