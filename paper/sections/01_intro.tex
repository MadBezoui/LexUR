\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The Pareto front as a safety filter, not a decision object}
Since the work of Pareto, efficiency has been the cornerstone of multicriteria
analysis: an alternative is Pareto efficient if no criterion can be improved
without worsening another. This is a logically impeccable \emph{safety
property}---it removes alternatives that are dominated and therefore indefensible.
It is, however, \emph{decisionally incomplete}. For $m$ conflicting criteria the
efficient set is typically large or infinite, and the decision maker (DM) is left
with a cloud of efficient alternatives and no principled rule for selecting one.

Confronted with this gap, practice resorts to \emph{post hoc} selection devices,
each of which reintroduces the very preference information the DM was unable to
supply: a weighted sum requires weights and cannot reach non-convex parts of the
front \citep{miettinen1999}; TOPSIS \citep{hwang1981} requires a distance metric
and weights; compromise programming \citep{zeleny1982} requires a metric exponent
and weights; knee-point rules \citep{branke2008} are scale sensitive; outranking
methods such as ELECTRE \citep{roy1991} and PROMETHEE \citep{brans2016} require
concordance/discordance thresholds; and hypervolume-based selection
\citep{zitzler1999} favours extremes and is costly. Every one of these asks the
DM to make \emph{further} choices \emph{after} optimisation---choices often no
easier than the original problem.

\subsection{From a front to a certificate}
We argue that, for the purpose of \emph{choosing}, the efficient set is the wrong
final object. The right object is a \emph{regret certificate}: a vector of
normalised disappointments over the rational questions the DM might reasonably
ask about the criteria, sorted from worst to best. Rather than asking ``which
alternatives are efficient?'' we ask
\begin{quote}
\emph{which alternative is least fragile across all declared rational
interpretations of the criteria?}
\end{quote}
This turns the output of the analysis from a \emph{set} into a \emph{certified
recommendation}: a single alternative (or small indifference class) accompanied
by an explanation of which questions are most disappointed and by how much.

\subsection{The Leximax Universal-Regret core}
We formalise this as the \textbf{Leximax Universal-Regret (LUR) core}. Given a
feasible set and a \emph{declared} class $\Q$ of admissible monotone probes
(scalar ``rational questions'' over the normalised criteria), LUR ranks
alternatives by the lexicographically sorted vector of their normalised regrets
across $\Q$, and returns the lexicographic minimiser together with its
certificate. The construction rests on three principles: (i) \emph{declared
admissibility}---$\Q$ is part of the problem statement, not hidden inside a
scalarisation; (ii) \emph{Pareto compatibility}---under monotone probes a
dominated alternative is never preferred to its dominator; and (iii) \emph{direct
computability}---the recommendation is obtained by a sequence of minimax-regret
problems, without enumerating the efficient set.

\subsection{Positioning and contribution}
LUR belongs to \emph{robust} multicriteria decision analysis. Its closest
relatives are stochastic multicriteria acceptability analysis (SMAA)
\citep{lahdelma1998,tervonen2007}, which integrates acceptability over a
preference distribution; robust ordinal regression
\citep{greco2008,figueira2009}, which reasons over the set of value functions
compatible with elicited holistic judgements; and preference robust optimisation
\citep{armbruster2015,hu2015}, which optimises against an ambiguity set of utility
functions. LUR differs from all three in object and output: it computes the
\emph{lexicographic minimax of normalised regret} over an explicit, monotone
probe family and returns an interpretable certificate; it requires no preference
distribution, no elicited judgements, and no single scalarisation. We show that
achievement scalarisation \citep{wierzbicki1980}, the weighted sum, and the
lexicographic maximin \citep{ogryczak1997} are recovered as special cases of the
probe family, so LUR is a \emph{unifying} as well as a new rule.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{C1}] \textbf{The LUR order:} a complete, Pareto-respecting preorder
on feasible alternatives based on lexicographic minimax regret over a declared
class of monotone probes (\S\ref{sec:core}).
\item[\textbf{C2}] \textbf{Guarantees:} existence and completeness, Pareto
compatibility \emph{under an explicit separation condition} (it is enough that
$\Q$ contains the singleton probes), dominated-solution exclusion, and stability
of the tolerance-based leximax rule under bounded regret perturbations, with full
proofs (\S\ref{sec:core}, Appendix~A).
\item[\textbf{C3}] \textbf{Direct computation:} a sequential and a single-stage
minimax-regret formulation that avoids efficient-set enumeration, with a smoothed
variant for continuous feasible sets (\S\ref{sec:direct}).
\item[\textbf{C4}] \textbf{Adaptive redundancy-aware probes:} a
correlation-clustering construction that keeps the probe count linear in $m$ and
the number of clusters, with a perturbation bound; empirically it reduces the
redundancy sensitivity that distorts averaging methods (\S\ref{sec:probes}).
\item[\textbf{C5}] \textbf{Extensions:} confidence-aware stochastic certificates
and a Rawlsian multi-stakeholder variant (\S\ref{sec:ext}).
\item[\textbf{C6}] \textbf{Held-out evaluation:} a replicated study against
preference models \emph{not} used during selection, plus a stochastic smart-grid
dispatch case, with non-parametric significance testing and a full reproducibility
package (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper reviews related work (\S\ref{sec:related}), develops
the core and its guarantees (\S\ref{sec:core}), the direct formulation
(\S\ref{sec:direct}), the adaptive probes (\S\ref{sec:probes}) and the extensions
(\S\ref{sec:ext}), reports the computational study (\S\ref{sec:exp}), discusses
limitations (\S\ref{sec:disc}) and concludes (\S\ref{sec:conc}). All proofs are
in Appendix~A.
