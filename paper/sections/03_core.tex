\label{sec:core}

\subsection{Problem setting and normalisation}
Let $\X\subseteq\R^n$ be the feasible set and $F:\X\to\R^m$ the vector objective to be minimised. We consider a candidate set $A=\{x_1,\dots,x_N\}\subseteq\X$. Criteria are normalised to $[0,1]$ by the positive affine map
\begin{equation}
r_i(x)=\frac{f_i(x)-\ideal_i}{\nadir_i-\ideal_i},\qquad
\ideal_i=\min_{x\in A}f_i(x),\;\; \nadir_i=\max_{x\in A}f_i(x),
\label{eq:norm}
\end{equation}
which attains both endpoints on $A$. Degenerate criteria (where $\nadir_i-\ideal_i \approx 0$) are excluded from non-singleton probe construction unless external bounds are provided. Because active-set normalisation is set-dependent, LexUR should be used with externally fixed bounds or accompanied by a stability analysis.

\subsection{Admissible probes and disappointment}
A \emph{probe} $q:[0,1]^m\to\R$ encodes a rational decision question. The family $\Q$ is explicitly declared.

\begin{definition}[Admissible probe]
\label{def:probe}
A probe $q$ is admissible if it is non-decreasing in each $r_i$ (monotonicity) and can be rescaled such that $q(\mathbf 0)=0$ and $q(\mathbf 1)=1$ (normalisation).
\end{definition}

Canonical probes include singletons $q_i(r)=r_i$, the grand mean $q_\mu(r)=\frac1m\sum_i r_i$, and the grand maximum $q_{\max}(r)=\max_i r_i$. Monotonicity ensures weak Pareto compatibility, while strict compatibility requires the family to \emph{separate} criteria (e.g., by including all singleton probes). The multiplicity of probes in $\Q$ is a deliberate modelling choice, as repeated probes weight the lexicographic sorting.

For each probe $q\in\Q$, let $q^\star=\min_{x\in A}q(r(x))$ and $q^{\mathrm w}=\max_{x\in A}q(r(x))$ be its active extrema. If the active range $R_q = q^{\mathrm w} - q^\star$ is strictly positive, the \emph{normalised disappointment} of $x$ under $q$ is
\begin{equation}
\reg_q(x)=\frac{q(r(x))-q^\star}{R_q}\in[0,1].
\label{eq:disap}
\end{equation}
The regret certificate is the vector of these probe-wise disappointments. 

\subsection{The leximax order}
Collect the disappointments $\reg(x)=(\reg_{q_1}(x),\dots,\reg_{q_K}(x))$ and sort them in descending order to obtain $\reg^{\downarrow}(x)$. The Leximax Universal-Regret order is defined as
\begin{equation}
x\preceq_{\mathrm{LexUR}}y \iff \reg^{\downarrow}(x)\lex \reg^{\downarrow}(y).
\label{eq:lexur}
\end{equation}
The method compares sorted regret vectors from worst coordinate downward until the first strict difference. The LexUR solution $x^{\mathrm{LexUR}}$ minimises this order, returning a certificate identifying the probes attaining the largest binding regrets.

In practice, a tolerance $\tau>0$ absorbs estimation noise. We define $W_\tau$ as the set of $\tau$-undominated alternatives. It acts as a numerical reporting device rather than a transitive equivalence class.

\subsection{Interval bounds and stability}
To address sensitivity to bound estimates, robustness requires evaluating regrets over a box of plausible ideal and nadir intervals $\mathcal B$. We define two fundamental stability sets: the \emph{necessary interval winner} set $S_{\text{necessary}}^\tau=\bigcap_{b\in\mathcal B} W_\tau(b)$ (options winning under all bounds), and the \emph{possible interval winner} set $S_{\text{possible}}^\tau=\bigcup_{b\in\mathcal B} W_\tau(b)$ (options winning under some bound). When bounds are highly uncertain, these stability classes should be reported instead of a single fragile point recommendation.

\subsection{Foundational guarantees}

\begin{theorem}[Existence and completeness]
\label{thm:exist}
For any finite $A$ and finite $\Q$, $x^{\mathrm{LexUR}}$ exists and exact $\preceq_{\mathrm{LexUR}}$ is a complete preorder on $A$.
\end{theorem}
The standard lexicographic operator guarantees a well-defined complete ranking on finite sets.

\begin{theorem}[Weak and strict Pareto compatibility]
\label{thm:pareto}
Let $x$ strictly Pareto-dominate $y$ and let every $q\in\Q$ be monotone non-decreasing in each $r_i$. Then $\reg_q(x)\le\reg_q(y)$ for all $q$, so $x\preceq_{\mathrm{LexUR}}y$. If $\Q$ separates criteria, then $x\prec_{\mathrm{LexUR}}y$.
\end{theorem}
Monotonicity alone prevents strict inversion but permits ties. Separation breaks the tie, strictly enforcing the Pareto principle.

\begin{corollary}[Dominated exclusion]
\label{cor:dom}
If $x \in A$ is Pareto-dominated by some $y \in A$, and $\Q$ separates criteria, then $x$ is not LexUR-optimal over $A$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Stability of the implemented winner]
\label{thm:stab}
Let $\hat\reg_q(x)$ estimate $\reg_q(x)$ with $\eta=\max_{x,q}|\hat\reg_q(x)-\reg_q(x)|$. Suppose the exact winner $x^{\mathrm{LexUR}}$ beats every rival $y$ at the first deciding coordinate with margin $\Delta$. If $2\eta<\min\{\Delta-\tau,\ \tau\}$, the tolerance-based winner is unchanged.
\end{theorem}
This guarantees that small perturbations cannot flip the recommendation provided the true margin exceeds the estimation noise.
