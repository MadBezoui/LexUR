\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

Table~\ref{tab:positioning} compactly maps LexUR against the standard families of multicriteria decision analysis. Broadly, existing selection devices fall into four categories: utility aggregation (e.g., weighted sum, OWA \citep{yager1988,torra1997}), distance to reference rules (e.g., TOPSIS \citep{hwang1981}, CP \citep{zeleny1982}, ASF \citep{wierzbicki1980}), regret-based rules \citep{savage1951,kouvelis1997}, and stochastic acceptability or robustness frameworks. The classical methods are robust but typically require elicited weights, thresholds, metrics, or explicit reference points, as detailed in the comparative table.

LexUR aligns most closely with robust decision analysis, particularly SMAA \citep{lahdelma1998,tervonen2007}, robust ordinal regression (ROR) \citep{greco2008,figueira2009}, and preference robust optimisation (PRO) \citep{armbruster2015,hu2015}. However, LexUR's operational mechanics and outputs are distinct. Rather than returning an acceptability index over a weight distribution (SMAA), reasoning over elicited value functions (ROR), or optimising a worst-case expected utility (PRO), LexUR optimises the lexicographically sorted vector of normalised regrets. Crucially, it operates over an explicit, declared family of monotone questions (probes) and outputs an auditable certificate that names the binding coalitions without requiring elicited preferences or sampling assumptions. 

LexUR does not subsume or replace Pareto analysis. It is a prescriptive rule that turns a feasible candidate set into a robust, explainable recommendation under declared monotone probes. It recovers specific methods like minimax regret or ASF only under restricted, singleton probe families. Ultimately, its distinguishing contribution is formulating decision-making as a lexicographic minimax over normalised disappointments, supplemented by an adaptive redundancy-aware probe construction and an interpretable regret certificate.
