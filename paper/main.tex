\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Pareto optimality is a safety property, not a complete decision rule: an
efficient set tells a decision maker (DM) which alternatives are not obviously
bad, but offers no principled way to choose one. Practice fills this gap with
\emph{post hoc} devices---weighted sums, TOPSIS, compromise programming,
knee selection, outranking thresholds---each of which reintroduces exactly the
preference parameters the DM was unable to state. We propose the
\textbf{Leximax Universal-Regret (LUR) core}, a Pareto-compatible decision rule
that ranks feasible alternatives by the lexicographically sorted vector of
\emph{normalised regrets over a declared class of monotone probes}, and returns
a single recommendation together with an auditable \emph{regret certificate}
identifying the most disappointed coalitions of criteria. We position LUR within
robust multicriteria decision analysis. It recovers achievement scalarisation,
the weighted sum, and the lexicographic maximin as special cases when the probe
family is reduced to the corresponding single probe, and it relates to---but is
operationally distinct from---stochastic multicriteria acceptability analysis
(SMAA), robust ordinal regression, and preference robust optimisation. We prove
existence and completeness, Pareto compatibility \emph{under a separation
condition}, dominated-solution exclusion, stability of the tolerance-based
leximax rule under bounded perturbations, and a probe perturbation bound with
explicit constants, and we give an adaptive correlation-clustering construction
that keeps the probe count linear in the number of criteria. A replicated study
(four front geometries, $m\in\{3,5,8,10\}$, 30 replications, 480 instances) and a
six-objective stochastic smart-grid dispatch case evaluate every method against
six held-out preference families, both additive and non-additive. On worst-case
(tail) held-out loss LUR is practically equivalent to the best robust
scalarisations (negligible effect size, Cliff's $|\delta|\le0.03$) and reduces
loss by $11$--$47\%$ relative to the distance-based and Monte-Carlo preference
methods used in practice, while (i) resisting redundant criteria, (ii) minimising
its certificate regret by construction, and (iii) admitting native stochastic and
multi-stakeholder extensions. We are explicit about scope: LUR relocates
preference modelling into a declared probe family rather than removing it, and
does not dominate the strongest robust scalarisations on average-case loss. All
code, data and scripts that regenerate every table and figure are released.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Decision support; Multicriteria decision analysis;
Robust decision making; Minimax regret; Achievement scalarising functions;
Pareto efficiency.
